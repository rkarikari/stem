CHAPTER 5

🛰️ Amateur Radio Satellite Communications

Amateur radio satellites (OSCAR - Orbiting Satellite Carrying Amateur Radio) provide exciting opportunities for long-distance communication, experimentation with space technology, and practical application of orbital mechanics. This chapter covers everything from basic orbital theory to making your first satellite contact.

Orbital Mechanics Fundamentals

Kepler's Laws of Planetary Motion

Law 1: Law of Ellipses

All satellites orbit in elliptical paths with Earth at one focus. Most amateur satellites use near-circular orbits (low eccentricity < 0.01) for consistent coverage.

Law 2: Law of Equal Areas

A line connecting the satellite to Earth sweeps out equal areas in equal times. This means satellites move faster at perigee (closest point to Earth) and slower at apogee (farthest point). For circular orbits, speed is constant.

Law 3: Law of Periods

The square of the orbital period is proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis:

T² ∝ a³

Practical formula for calculating orbital period:

T (minutes) = 2π × √(a³ / μ)

where:

  • T = orbital period in minutes
  • a = semi-major axis (Earth radius + altitude) in kilometers
  • μ = 398,600.4 km³/s² (Earth's standard gravitational parameter)
  • π = 3.14159...

Example Calculation: SO-124 Orbital Period

SO-124 (HADES-R) orbits at approximately 550 km altitude above Earth's surface.

  • Earth radius (R) = 6,371 km
  • Altitude (h) = 550 km
  • Semi-major axis (a) = R + h = 6,371 + 550 = 6,921 km

T = 2π × √(6,921³ / 398,600.4)
T = 6.283 × √(331,400,000,000 / 398,600.4)
T = 6.283 × √(831,400)
T = 6.283 × 911.8
T = 5,730 seconds
T = 95.5 minutes ≈ 1 hour 35.5 minutes

This matches SO-124's actual orbital period of approximately 96 minutes.

Two-Line Element Sets (TLE)

TLEs are standardized data formats that precisely describe a satellite's orbit. They are updated regularly (daily to weekly) as orbits decay due to atmospheric drag and solar radiation pressure.

Understanding Orbital Parameters

Parameter Meaning Impact on Operations
Inclination Angle between orbital plane and equator (0-180°) • 0°: Equatorial orbit (no high latitude coverage)
• 90°: Polar orbit (global coverage)
• 97.7° (AO-91): Sun-synchronous retrograde orbit
• High inclination = better global coverage
Eccentricity Shape of orbit (0 = circular, >0 = elliptical) • 0.000 to 0.010: Nearly circular (most amateur sats)
• 0.010 to 0.100: Slightly elliptical
• Low eccentricity = consistent coverage and power
Mean Motion Orbits completed per day • 14-16 orbits/day: Typical LEO (700-1000 km altitude)
• Higher mean motion = lower orbit = faster passes
• Directly determines pass frequency
RAAN Longitude where orbit crosses equator northbound • Changes daily due to Earth's rotation
• Determines which longitudes get passes
• Updated TLEs needed for accurate predictions
Mean Anomaly Satellite's position in orbit at epoch time • 0°: At perigee
• 180°: At apogee
• Used to calculate current position

Calculating Pass Predictions from TLE

Satellite tracking software uses the SGP4 (Simplified General Perturbations-4) propagator model to convert TLE data into real-time satellite positions. Here's the process:

Pass Prediction Algorithm Flow:

  1. Load TLE: Parse the two lines into orbital elements
  2. Set Observer Location: Define ground station coordinates (latitude, longitude, altitude)
  3. Propagate Orbit: Use SGP4 to calculate satellite position at current time
    • Output: Satellite position (x, y, z) in Earth-Centered Inertial (ECI) coordinates
    • Output: Satellite velocity vector
  4. Convert to Topocentric Coordinates: Calculate position relative to observer
    • Azimuth (0-360°): Compass bearing from observer to satellite
    • Elevation (-90° to +90°): Angle above horizon
    • Range (km): Direct distance to satellite
  5. Determine Pass Times:
    • AOS (Acquisition of Signal): When elevation rises above minimum (typically 5-10°)
    • TCA (Time of Closest Approach): When elevation reaches maximum
    • LOS (Loss of Signal): When elevation drops below minimum
  6. Calculate Doppler Shift: Use velocity vector to determine frequency changes
  7. Repeat for Next Orbits: Generate predictions for upcoming passes

Doppler Effect in Satellite Communications

Understanding Doppler Shift

The Doppler effect causes frequency shifts due to the relative motion between satellite and observer. As a satellite approaches (closing velocity), the received frequency increases. As it recedes (opening velocity), frequency decreases. This effect can shift signals by ±3.5 kHz on VHF and ±10 kHz on UHF.

Doppler Frequency Calculation

freceived = ftransmitted × (1 + vradial / c)

Where:

  • freceived = frequency you observe at ground station
  • ftransmitted = satellite's actual transmission frequency
  • vradial = component of satellite velocity toward/away from observer (m/s)
    • Positive when approaching (satellite moving toward you)
    • Negative when receding (satellite moving away)
    • Zero at TCA (satellite directly overhead, perpendicular motion)
  • c = speed of light = 3 × 108 m/s

Typical LEO Satellite Doppler Shifts:

Pass Phase 2m VHF (145 MHz) 70cm UHF (435 MHz) Radial Velocity
AOS (Rising) +3.5 kHz +10.5 kHz +7,200 m/s (approaching)
30° Elevation +3.0 kHz +9.0 kHz +6,200 m/s
60° Elevation +1.5 kHz +4.5 kHz +3,100 m/s
TCA (Overhead) 0 Hz 0 Hz 0 m/s (perpendicular)
60° Elevation -1.5 kHz -4.5 kHz -3,100 m/s
30° Elevation -3.0 kHz -9.0 kHz -6,200 m/s
LOS (Setting) -3.5 kHz -10.5 kHz -7,200 m/s (receding)

Key Observations:

  • Total Doppler Swing: 7 kHz on VHF, 21 kHz on UHF (AOS to LOS)
  • Rate of Change: Fastest near horizon, slowest at TCA
  • Frequency Dependency: UHF shift is 3× larger than VHF shift (frequency ratio)
  • Symmetric: Positive shift magnitude at AOS equals negative magnitude at LOS

Doppler Compensation Strategies

1. Manual Tuning (FM Satellites)

Procedure for Manual Doppler Correction:

  1. Pre-Pass Setup:
    • Program radio with nominal downlink frequency
    • Have Gpredict or tracking software open with Doppler display
    • Note expected Doppler range for this pass
  2. At AOS (Satellite Rising):
    • Set receiver frequency HIGH: Nominal + 3.5 kHz
    • Example: SO-124 downlink = 436.885 MHz + 3.5 kHz = 436.8885 MHz
    • Listen for satellite beacon or other stations
  3. During Pass (Tracking):
    • Continuously adjust frequency DOWNWARD as satellite passes overhead
    • Typical adjustment: 0.5-1.0 kHz every 30-60 seconds
    • Keep signal centered in receiver passband (listen for clear audio)
    • At TCA (overhead): frequency = nominal (436.885 MHz exactly)
  4. At LOS (Satellite Setting):
    • Final frequency LOW: Nominal - 3.5 kHz
    • Example: 436.885 MHz - 3.5 kHz = 436.8815 MHz
    • Total adjustment from AOS to LOS: 7 kHz

Uplink Doppler (Less Critical on FM):

  • Doppler shift is inverted on uplink vs downlink
  • At AOS: Transmit LOW (145.925 - 10 kHz = 145.915 MHz)
  • At LOS: Transmit HIGH (145.925 + 10 kHz = 145.935 MHz)
  • FM mode is more forgiving - ±5 kHz deviation masks small Doppler errors
  • Most operators don't adjust uplink Doppler on FM satellites

2. Computer-Aided Doppler Correction

Software like Gpredict, SatPC32, or Ham Radio Deluxe can automatically adjust radio frequencies via CAT (Computer Aided Transceiver) interface.

Setup Requirements:

  • Radio: Must support CAT control (most modern transceivers do)
    • Icom: CI-V interface
    • Yaesu: CAT/Serial interface
    • Kenwood: PC interface
  • Connection: USB-to-serial cable or built-in USB
  • Software: Gpredict (free, Linux/Windows/Mac) or SatPC32 (Windows, $$)
  • Configuration:
    • Set radio model in software
    • Configure COM port and baud rate
    • Enable Doppler correction
    • Set update rate (1-2 seconds typical)

Operation:

  1. Software calculates real-time satellite position and velocity
  2. Computes instantaneous Doppler shift for TX and RX frequencies
  3. Sends frequency adjustment commands to radio via CAT
  4. Radio automatically tracks changing frequency throughout pass
  5. Operator focuses on making contacts, not tuning

Advantages:

  • Precise Doppler correction (±10 Hz accuracy)
  • Hands-free operation - no manual tuning needed
  • Essential for SSB/CW linear transponder work
  • Reduces operator workload during busy passes

Satellite Tracking Software

Gpredict - Free Open-Source Tracking

Gpredict is the most popular free satellite tracking software, available for Linux, Windows, and macOS. It provides pass predictions, real-time tracking, radio control, and rotator interfaces.

Setting Up Gpredict

Step 1: Installation

  • Linux: sudo apt-get install gpredict
  • Windows: Download installer from http://gpredict.oz9aec.net
  • macOS: Use Homebrew: brew install gpredict

Step 2: Update TLE Data (Critical!)

  1. Menu: Edit → Update TLEs from Network
  2. Select "All" or specific satellites (SO-124, SO-50, IO-86, ISS)
  3. Click "Update" and wait for download
  4. Repeat weekly for accurate predictions (TLEs decay)

Step 3: Configure Ground Station Location

  1. Menu: Edit → Preferences → Ground Stations
  2. Click "Add New" button
  3. Enter station details:
    • Name: Your station callsign or location name
    • Latitude: In decimal degrees (e.g., 5.603 for Accra)
    • Longitude: In decimal degrees (e.g., -0.187 for Accra)
    • Altitude: Meters above sea level (e.g., 61 m for Accra)
  4. Click "OK" to save

Step 4: Create Satellite Module

  1. Menu: File → New Module
  2. Enter module name (e.g., "FM Satellites" or "My Satellites")
  3. In satellite list, select satellites to track:
    • SO-124 (62690)
    • SO-50 (27607)
    • IO-86 (40931)
    • ISS (25544)
    • Add any other satellites of interest
  4. Click "OK" - module opens with satellite tracks

Step 5: Configure Pass Prediction

  1. In module window, right-click satellite → Predict Passes
  2. Set parameters:
    • Start time: Now or specific date/time
    • Duration: 7 days typical (1 week of passes)
    • Minimum elevation: 10° recommended (5° for low horizon)
    • Ground station: Select your QTH from dropdown
  3. Click "Predict" - see list of upcoming passes
  4. Passes show:
    • AOS (Acquisition of Signal) time and azimuth
    • TCA (Time of Closest Approach) and max elevation
    • LOS (Loss of Signal) time and azimuth
    • Pass duration

Step 6: Radio Control (Optional)

  1. Menu: Edit → Preferences → Interfaces → Radios
  2. Add radio configuration:
    • Select radio model
    • Configure COM port and baud rate
    • Enable Doppler correction
    • Set update rate (1-2 seconds)
  3. In module, click "Radio Control" button
  4. Select satellite and engage tracking
  5. Software automatically tunes radio throughout pass

  • ✅ Select pass with >30° max elevation and >8 min duration
  • ✅ Check satellite status (AMSAT.org/status) - confirm satellite is operational
  • ✅ Verify weather forecast (clear skies preferred for first attempt)
  • ✅ Charge all batteries (radio, phone/tablet for tracking)

1 Hour Before Pass:

  • ✅ Program radio memories:
    • Memory A (RX): Downlink frequency (e.g., 436.885 MHz for SO-124)
    • Memory B (TX): Uplink frequency (145.925 MHz, no CTCSS needed)
  • ✅ Test radio:
    • Verify TX power (5W recommended)
    • Check squelch setting (slightly open to hear weak signals)
  • ✅ Prepare script:
    • "CQ satellite CQ satellite, this is [your callsign], [grid square], listening"
    • Example: "CQ satellite CQ satellite, this is N0CALL, JJ05, listening"
  • ✅ Set up logging:
    • Paper and pencil ready
    • Log format: Time, Callsign, Grid, Signal Report, Notes
  • ✅ Scout location:
    • Find clear area with unobstructed view of sky
    • Note AOS azimuth direction (where satellite will rise)
    • Identify landmarks for directional reference

5 Minutes Before AOS:

  • ✅ Start tracking app
  • ✅ Orient antenna toward AOS azimuth (where satellite will appear)
  • ✅ Set receiver frequency HIGH (+3 kHz above nominal for Doppler)
  • ✅ Squelch slightly open
  • ✅ Volume at comfortable level
  • ✅ Take deep breath - relax and enjoy!

During the Pass: Step-by-Step Procedure

Phase 1: Acquisition (AOS → +2 minutes)

  1. Listen First (Critical!):
    • Tune receiver to downlink + 3 kHz (e.g., 436.8885 MHz for SO-124)
    • Point antenna low at horizon in AOS direction
    • Listen for satellite beacon or other stations
    • Typical beacon: "HADES-R telemetry" (repeated)
    • If you hear nothing at AOS time, slowly sweep ±5 kHz
  2. Signal Acquired:
    • Once you hear satellite, note signal strength
    • Begin tracking: adjust antenna to follow satellite path
    • Start frequency tuning downward (Doppler compensation)
    • DO NOT TRANSMIT YET - listen for activity

Phase 2: Calling & Making Contact (TCA -3 min → TCA +3 min)

  1. Wait for Break in Traffic:
    • Satellite is SHARED resource - hundreds may be listening
    • Wait for 2-3 second gap with no transmissions
    • Best time to call: 30-45° elevation (signal strong, not too crowded)
  2. Make Your Call (Keep It BRIEF!):
    • Initial call (2-3 seconds max): "[Callsign], [Grid]"
    • Example: "N0CALL, JJ05"
    • DO NOT say: "This is...", "calling CQ...", "standing by..." - NO TIME!
    • Just: Callsign + Grid. That's it.
  3. Listen for Response:
    • Someone might respond with your callsign
    • Example response: "N0CALL, W1ABC, FN31"
    • Log their callsign and grid immediately
  4. Complete the Exchange (Minimal!):
    • Acknowledge: "W1ABC, N0CALL, 59, thanks, 73"
    • Translation: "I heard you (5x9 signal report), thank you, goodbye"
    • Total exchange: Under 5 seconds
    • DO NOT RAGCHEW! Others need access too.

Phase 3: Tracking & Additional Contacts

  1. Continue Tracking:
    • Continuously point antenna at satellite (follow tracking app)
    • Adjust receiver frequency downward (tune through Doppler)
    • Maximum elevation = best signal = most activity
    • Can make multiple contacts during same pass
  2. Try Again if Time Allows:
    • After completing one contact, wait 10-15 seconds
    • If clear, call again: "N0CALL, JJ05"
    • Work as many stations as possible before LOS

Phase 4: Loss of Signal (LOS)

  1. Signal Fading:
    • As satellite approaches horizon, signal weakens
    • Receiver frequency will be ~3 kHz LOW of nominal
    • Point antenna low toward LOS azimuth
    • Signal will fade into noise
  2. Post-Pass Activities:
    • Immediately log all contacts with timestamps
    • Upload to satellite logging sites (ARRL LoTW, QRZ.com)
    • Send QSL cards via satellite bureau (optional)
    • Review performance - what worked well? What to improve?

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • ❌ Talking Too Much: "CQ CQ this is November Zero Charlie Alpha Lima Lima, calling CQ satellite..." - WRONG! Just say callsign + grid.
  • ❌ Using Phonetics: Unless absolutely necessary due to interference. They waste precious time.
  • ❌ Transmitting Over Others: Wait for clear frequency. Collisions destroy everyone's contacts.
  • ❌ Transmitting Over Beacon: If you hear telemetry beacon, frequency is busy - WAIT.
  • ❌ Forgetting to Check Status: SO-124 is new but other satellites may have schedules or issues - always check AMSAT status.
  • ❌ Not Tracking Frequency: Doppler will move signal out of receiver passband - keep tuning!
  • ❌ Wrong Polarization: Linear antenna 90° off = 20+ dB signal loss. Rotate antenna if signal weak.
  • ❌ Excessive Power: Don't run 50W into FM satellite - causes problems for everyone. 5-10W is plenty.
  • ❌ Not Listening First: Always acquire signal and assess activity before transmitting.
  • ❌ Calling on Inactive Satellite: Always verify satellite is operational before attempting contact.

Linear Transponder Operation (SSB/CW)

Linear transponders are more advanced than FM satellites. They translate an entire band of frequencies (typically 20-100 kHz wide) from uplink to downlink, allowing multiple simultaneous QSOs.

Key Differences from FM Satellites

Aspect FM Satellites Linear Transponders
Modulation FM (Frequency Modulation) SSB (Single Sideband) or CW (Morse code)
Number of QSOs One at a time (repeater) Multiple simultaneous (like HF band)
Passband Single frequency 20-100 kHz band (many frequencies)
Inversion None USB uplink becomes LSB downlink (or vice versa)
Full Duplex Optional (helpful) ESSENTIAL - must monitor own downlink signal
Power Control 5-10W typical 1-5W MAX - more power = harm to all users (ALC issues)
Doppler Important (3-10 kHz) CRITICAL - must pre-compensate uplink and track downlink
Operating Style Fast, brief exchanges Normal SSB QSO pace (but still brief for others)
Equipment Handheld can work Base station with dual VFO and better antennas required
Difficulty Beginner-friendly Intermediate to Advanced

Linear Transponder Operating Procedure

Step 1: Pre-Pass Setup

  • Radio Configuration:
    • VFO-A (Receive): Downlink frequency, LSB mode (for V/U transponders)
    • VFO-B (Transmit): Uplink frequency, USB mode
    • Enable full duplex / split operation
    • Reduce TX power to minimum (1-2W to start)
  • Frequency Planning:
    • Example for RS-44 (V/U transponder):
    • Uplink passband: 145.935 - 145.995 MHz (60 kHz wide)
    • Downlink passband: 435.610 - 435.670 MHz (inverted)
    • Relationship: 145.935 up → 435.670 down
    • Relationship: 145.995 up → 435.610 down
  • Doppler Pre-Compensation:
    • At AOS: Set uplink LOW (-10 kHz), downlink HIGH (+10 kHz)
    • At TCA: Uplink and downlink at nominal frequencies
    • At LOS: Set uplink HIGH (+10 kHz), downlink LOW (-10 kHz)
    • Use software to automate this (essential for success)

Step 2: Listen First

  1. At AOS, tune across entire downlink passband (435.610-435.670 MHz)
  2. Listen for beacon (usually at 435.605 MHz CW "RS44")
  3. Identify active QSOs and clear frequencies
  4. Note signal strengths and which parts of band are busy

Step 3: Find Yourself

  1. Select clear frequency on uplink (e.g., 145.965 MHz USB)
  2. Calculate corresponding downlink:
    • 145.965 is middle of 145.935-145.995 range
    • Middle of downlink: 435.640 MHz
    • Tune downlink to 435.640 MHz LSB
  3. Key up briefly (1 second) and say "Test"
  4. Listen on downlink - you should hear yourself!
  5. Adjust uplink Doppler until your signal appears at expected downlink frequency
  6. If too high or too low, adjust uplink frequency and try again

Step 4: Make Contact

  • Option A - Call CQ:
    • "CQ satellite, CQ satellite, November Zero Charlie Alpha Lima Lima, grid square Juliet Juliet Zero Five, calling CQ"
    • Listen on downlink for responses
  • Option B - Answer CQ:
    • When you hear someone calling CQ, tune your uplink to match their downlink
    • Wait for them to finish calling
    • Reply: "Whiskey One Alpha Bravo Charlie, this is November Zero Charlie Alpha Lima Lima"

Step 5: Complete QSO

  • Exchange signal reports: "You're 5-7 (slightly weak but readable)"
  • Exchange locations: "I'm in grid square Juliet Juliet Zero Five, Accra, Ghana"
  • Brief comments: "Thanks for the contact, 73"
  • Keep QSO under 2-3 minutes to allow others access
  • Continuously monitor own downlink - adjust TX power if too strong or weak

⚠️ Critical: Power Control on Linear Transponders

TOO MUCH POWER DAMAGES THE ENTIRE TRANSPONDER FOR ALL USERS!

The Problem: Linear transponders have Automatic Level Control (ALC) to prevent any one strong signal from capturing all available power. If you transmit with excessive power:

  • ✗ Transponder ALC reduces gain for EVERYONE
  • ✗ All other stations become weaker
  • ✗ Your signal may sound distorted
  • ✗ You become universally hated by satellite community
  • ✗ In extreme cases, transponder may shut down entirely

The Solution: MINIMUM POWER!

  • ✓ Start with 1-2 watts
  • ✓ Increase only if you cannot hear yourself on downlink
  • ✓ Never exceed 5 watts on uplink
  • ✓ If your downlink signal is stronger than others, REDUCE power immediately
  • ✓ Monitor your own signal continuously - this is why full duplex is essential

Rule of Thumb: If you can hear yourself clearly on the downlink, your power is sufficient. More power does NOT help - better antennas do!

ISS Operations

Voice Contacts with Astronauts

Talking directly to astronauts aboard the International Space Station is the "holy grail" of amateur satellite operations, but also one of the most challenging due to extreme competition.

⚠️ Reality Check: Voice Contacts Are VERY Rare

  • Astronaut Availability: Extremely limited. Crew members have full science schedules with minimal free time. Amateur radio operations happen only during rare breaks, typically on weekends.
  • Competition Level: When an astronaut does come on the air, HUNDREDS of stations worldwide are calling simultaneously. Success rate for individual stations is typically under 1%.
  • Time Constraints: ISS passes last only 8-12 minutes. Even if astronaut is on the air for full pass, they might complete only 10-15 contacts.
  • Current Status: As of 2025, voice operations happen sporadically. Check ARISS (Amateur Radio on ISS) website for scheduled events.

Operating Information:

Parameter Details
Callsign NA1SS (North America), typically used
Downlink (RX) 145.800 MHz FM (worldwide)
Uplink (TX) Region 2 & 3: 145.990 MHz FM
Region 1 (Europe/Africa): 144.490 MHz FM
Mode FM, no CTCSS tone required
Equipment Minimum: 25W, directional antenna
Recommended: 50-100W, high-gain Yagi with tracking
Etiquette • Listen first - is astronaut actually on the air?
• Call only once with callsign and location
• If no response after 2-3 attempts, STOP calling
• Keep exchange brief if you do make contact
• DO NOT ask for QSL card - they don't have time

Better Strategy: ARISS School Contacts are scheduled in advance. Schools can apply for contact opportunities. Check ariss.org for schedule. You can listen to these contacts - they're public!

ISS APRS Digipeater (Much More Reliable!)

The ISS APRS digipeater is operational nearly 24/7 and provides a much higher success rate than voice contacts.

How to Use ISS for APRS

What is APRS? Automatic Packet Reporting System - transmits GPS position, telemetry, and messages as digital packets on 144.390 MHz (North America) or 145.825 MHz (ISS operations).

Hardware Options:

  • Option 1: Dedicated APRS Radio
    • Kenwood TH-D74A or TM-D710G (built-in TNC and GPS)
    • Simply configure path as "ARISS" or "RS0ISS"
    • Radio handles everything automatically
    • Most reliable option
  • Option 2: External TNC
    • Any VHF radio + TNC (Terminal Node Controller)
    • Examples: Kantronics KPC-3, TinyTrak, Byonics TinyTrak4
    • Connect GPS to TNC, TNC to radio audio
    • Configure TNC with APRS settings
  • Option 3: Smartphone + Sound Card
    • APRSdroid (Android) or PocketPacket (iOS)
    • Audio cable from phone to radio
    • Phone GPS provides position
    • Software generates audio tones (SoftTNC)
    • Budget-friendly option

Configuration:

  • Frequency: 145.825 MHz FM (ISS worldwide frequency)
  • Path: ARISS or RS0ISS (try both - one usually works better)
  • Power: 5-25W (more helps but not essential)
  • Beacon Rate: Every 30-60 seconds during ISS pass
  • Symbol: Car, person, or house (whatever represents your station)
  • Comment: Optional short text (e.g., "RadioSport via ISS!")

Operation Procedure:

  1. Track ISS pass using ISS Detector or Gpredict
  2. 2 minutes before AOS, start transmitting APRS beacons every 30 sec
  3. Point antenna at ISS (handheld arrow antenna works)
  4. Continue beaconing throughout pass
  5. After pass, check APRS.fi to see if your packets were digipeated
  6. Success indicator: Your position appears on map with "via RS0ISS" in path

Example APRS Packet Format:

# Raw APRS packet transmitted to ISS N0CALL>APRS,ARISS:=0547.32N/00019.15W-RadioSport Mobile Station # Breakdown: # N0CALL = Your callsign # APRS = Destination (standard for APRS) # ARISS = Path (digipeater via ISS) # = = Position report with timestamp # 0547.32N/00019.15W = GPS coordinates (Accra area) # - = Symbol (car/mobile) # "RadioSport Mobile Station" = Comment field (optional) # After ISS digipeats your packet, ground IGates receive it and forward to internet # Your position then appears on https://aprs.fi with path showing "via RS0ISS"

Coverage Area:

  • ISS altitude: ~420 km
  • Radio horizon from ISS: ~2,300 km radius
  • Total footprint: ~16 million km² (about size of Russia!)
  • Much larger than terrestrial APRS digipeaters (typically 50-100 km radius)
  • Your packets can be received by IGates 1000+ km away

Success Rate: 80-95% with proper setup and clear pass. Much more reliable than voice contacts!

ISS SSTV Events

Several times per year (typically 3-6 events annually), the ISS transmits Slow Scan Television (SSTV) images continuously for 2-3 days.

What is SSTV?

Slow Scan Television transmits still images as audio tones over voice frequencies. Unlike fast-scan TV (like regular television), SSTV sends one image over 72 seconds to 4 minutes, making it suitable for HF and VHF communication.

ISS SSTV Event Specifics:

  • Frequency: 145.800 MHz FM (same as voice downlink)
  • Mode: Usually PD120 or PD180 (2-3 minutes per image)
  • Duration: Continuous transmission for 48-72 hours
  • Images: Space-themed pictures, often commemorating events or anniversaries
  • Schedule: Announced on ARISS website 1-2 weeks in advance
  • Receive-Only: No transmit needed - just listen and decode!

Equipment Needed:

  • Radio: Any VHF receiver (even scanner)
  • Antenna: Simple 2m dipole or handheld works
  • Computer: Laptop or PC with sound card
  • Audio Cable: Radio speaker/headphone output to computer line-in/microphone
  • Software:
    • Windows: MMSSTV (most popular, free)
    • Mac: Multiscan 3B (free)
    • Linux: QSSTV (free)
    • Android: Robot36 (free)
    • iOS: SSTV Slow Scan TV (paid app)

Reception Procedure:

  1. Tune Radio: 145.800 MHz FM, squelch open
  2. Track ISS: Use ISS Detector or similar app
  3. Start Software: Set to Auto mode (detects SSTV mode automatically)
  4. At AOS: Point antenna at ISS
  5. Listen: You'll hear characteristic SSTV tones (warbling sounds)
  6. Decode: Software displays image line-by-line as it's received
  7. Save Image: Software saves completed images with timestamp
  8. Repeat: Each pass may yield 1-3 images depending on duration

Tips for Best Results:

  • ✓ High passes (>30° elevation) yield better quality images
  • ✓ Point antenna continuously at ISS throughout pass
  • ✓ Adjust audio levels - too loud causes distortion, too quiet misses data
  • ✓ Watch for fading - partial images are common, try multiple passes
  • ✓ SSTV modes switch automatically in software - don't change manually
  • ✓ Save all images even if incomplete - partial decodes are still achievements!

ARISS SSTV Award Program:

ARISS issues certificates for received ISS SSTV images:

  • Receive and decode images during official SSTV event
  • Save images with timestamp visible
  • Submit images to ARISS (details on ariss.org during event)
  • Certificate mailed after event concludes
  • Multiple participation levels (1 image, 5 images, 10+ images)

Satellite Communication via Drone Relay

🚁 Advanced Concept: Drone-Assisted Satellite Reception

Problem Statement: Ground station has terrain blockage (mountains, buildings, trees) preventing low-elevation satellite passes. This reduces number of workable passes and eliminates some satellites entirely.

Solution: Lightweight drone carries receiver to altitude above terrain obstacles, relays signal to ground station, extending effective horizon and enabling passes that would otherwise be impossible.

System Architecture:

Airborne Segment:

  • Drone Platform: DJI Mavic Pro or Phantom 4
    • Phantom 4: 1375g empty, 125g payload capacity, 25 min flight time
    • Mavic Pro: 743g empty, 257g payload capacity, 22 min flight time with load
    • Both have GPS + GLONASS for precise positioning
    • Obstacle avoidance sensors for safety
  • Receiver Payload (~105g total):
    • RTL-SDR dongle (50g) - receives VHF/UHF satellite signals
    • GPS module (10g) - precise position and time sync
    • Raspberry Pi Zero W (15g) - processes signals and runs tracking software
    • Amplified 2m/70cm antenna (30g) - small rubber duck or circular polarized patch
    • MicroSD card for data logging
    • Battery (if separate from drone) or powered from drone via regulator
  • Mounting:
    • 3D-printed cradle attached to drone landing gear or gimbal mount
    • Antenna oriented vertically or with circular polarization
    • Weight distribution critical - must maintain drone balance
    • Secure attachment to prevent loss during flight

Ground Segment:

  • Drone Control: Standard DJI remote controller (2.4 GHz or 5.8 GHz)
  • Telemetry Downlink: Separate 433 MHz or 915 MHz link for receiver data
    • Raspberry Pi Zero transmits decoded audio or raw IQ samples
    • Ground station receives via USB radio dongle
    • Bandwidth: 50-200 kbps sufficient for audio
  • Tracking Computer: Laptop running Gpredict for satellite predictions
  • Recording: Audio recording software to capture contacts

Operating Procedure:

  1. Pre-Flight Planning (15 min before satellite AOS):
    • Check satellite pass prediction (elevation, azimuth, duration)
    • Verify weather conditions (wind <10 m/s, no precipitation)
    • Calculate optimal drone altitude (100-150m typically)
    • Plan flight path: climb before AOS, hover during pass, descend after LOS
    • Pre-flight drone inspection: battery charge, propellers, payload secure
  2. Launch & Positioning (5 min before AOS):
    • Launch drone from clear area
    • Climb to planned altitude (100m typical)
    • Position drone in direction of satellite AOS (if directional antenna)
    • Enable position hold mode (GPS hover)
    • Start satellite receiver and data logging
  3. During Pass (8-12 minutes):
    • Drone maintains altitude and position (autonomous hover)
    • Onboard receiver tracks satellite:
      • Option A: Manual Doppler tracking (pre-programmed frequency schedule)
      • Option B: Autonomous tracking using onboard Gpredict + TLE data
    • Receiver captures satellite signals (downlink only)
    • Data relayed to ground in real-time via telemetry link
    • Ground operator monitors reception quality and logs contacts
    • All data also recorded onboard for post-flight analysis
  4. Post-Pass & Recovery:
    • After satellite LOS, command drone to descend
    • Return to launch point (RTH)
    • Land safely
    • Download recorded data from microSD card
    • Analyze signal strength vs. altitude, position, and time
    • Compare received contacts vs. ground-based reception

Benefits of Drone-Based Satellite Reception:

  • Extend Horizon: Radio horizon from 100m altitude = 41 km vs. 5 km from ground
    • Can receive satellites at 5° elevation that ground station cannot
    • Adds 2-4 minutes to each pass duration
    • Makes more passes accessible overall
  • Terrain Clearance: Eliminates ground-level obstacles
    • Buildings no longer block signals
    • Trees and foliage not an issue
    • Mountains and hills cleared
    • Urban canyon effect eliminated
  • Mobile Operations: Deploy from vehicles or remote locations
    • Quick setup (5-10 minutes)
    • No need to find "perfect" site with clear horizon
    • Can operate from school parking lot, urban park, etc.
  • Research Platform: Unique data collection opportunities
    • Measure signal strength vs. altitude profile
    • Study Fresnel zone clearance requirements empirically
    • Test antenna patterns in unobstructed environment
    • Validate propagation models
  • Educational Value: Integrates multiple STEM disciplines
    • Satellite communications + Drone operations
    • RF engineering + Aviation
    • Software development + Systems integration
    • Problem-solving + Innovation

Challenges & Limitations:

  • Flight Time: 15-20 minutes maximum with payload (vs. 8-12 min satellite pass)
    • Must account for climb/descend time
    • Battery reserve needed (minimum 30% for RTH)
    • Can only capture one pass per flight typically
    • Solution: Hot-swap batteries for consecutive passes
  • RF Interference: Drone motors and ESCs generate electrical noise
    • Can interfere with weak satellite signals
    • Solution: Shielding, ferrite beads on power lines, filtering
    • Solution: Separate receiver power supply from drone battery
    • Test interference levels before deployment
  • Antenna Pointing: Maintaining optimal antenna orientation
    • Drone attitude changes affect antenna pattern
    • Omnidirectional or circular polarized antennas preferred
    • Gimbal-mounted directional antenna possible but adds weight/complexity
    • Trade-off: simple omni antenna vs. high-gain tracking antenna
  • Data Relay Bandwidth: Real-time telemetry link capacity
    • Audio stream: 32-64 kbps sufficient (compressed)
    • Raw IQ samples: 2-10 Mbps (challenging for long-range telemetry)
    • Solution: Onboard recording + real-time audio relay hybrid
    • Compressed data sent during flight, full fidelity downloaded after
  • Regulatory Compliance: Multiple jurisdictions involved
    • Drone operation: Requires Part 107 (US) or equivalent certification
    • Airspace: Must operate in Class G airspace or obtain authorization
    • RF transmission from drone: Amateur radio rules apply to telemetry
    • Line-of-sight requirement: Must maintain visual contact with drone
    • Altitude limits: Typically 400 ft AGL (122m) maximum without waiver
  • Weather Dependency: More restrictive than ground operations
    • Wind: <10 m/s (22 mph) for safe operation
    • Rain: No flight (damages electronics, affects drone stability)
    • Temperature: Check drone operating range (-10°C to +40°C typical)
    • Visibility: Must maintain VLOS (500m minimum)

rise_time) * 86400 # seconds } else: return None # Pass too low except ValueError: return None # No pass found def predict_passes(self, sat_name, days=7, min_elevation=10): """Predict all passes for next N days""" passes = [] start_time = datetime.datetime.utcnow() end_time = start_time + datetime.timedelta(days=days) current_time = start_time while current_time < end_time: pass_info = self.predict_pass(sat_name, current_time, min_elevation) if pass_info: passes.append(pass_info) # Start searching after this pass ends current_time = pass_info['los_time'] + datetime.timedelta(minutes=5) else: # No pass found, advance by 1 hour and try again current_time += datetime.timedelta(hours=1) return passes # Example usage tracker = SatelliteTracker( observer_lat=5.603, # Accra, Ghana observer_lon=-0.187, observer_alt=61 ) # Load SO-124 TLE tracker.load_tle( "SO-124", "1 62690U 25009CK 25345.78916667 .00001234 00000-0 54321-4 0 9998", "2 62690 97.5123 265.4321 0009234 62.1234 298.5678 14.92345678901234" ) # Predict next 7 days of passes passes = tracker.predict_passes("SO-124", days=7, min_elevation=10) # Display results print(f"Found {len(passes)} passes for SO-124 in next 7 days:\n") for i, p in enumerate(passes, 1): duration_min = int(p['duration'] / 60) duration_sec = int(p['duration'] % 60) print(f"Pass {i}:") print(f" AOS: {p['aos_time']} at {p['aos_azimuth']:.1f}°") print(f" TCA: {p['tca_time']} at {p['max_elevation']:.1f}° elevation") print(f" LOS: {p['los_time']} at {p['los_azimuth']:.1f}°") print(f" Duration: {duration_min}:{duration_sec:02d}\n")

Project 2: Automated Doppler Tuning System

Objective:

Build a hardware/software system that automatically tunes your radio frequency to compensate for Doppler shift throughout a satellite pass, enabling hands-free operation.

Hardware Components:

Component Purpose Options
Microcontroller Run tracking software, control radio • Raspberry Pi 4 (full-featured)
• Arduino Mega (real-time control)
• ESP32 (WiFi/Bluetooth capable)
GPS Module Accurate time and position • u-blox NEO-6M ($15)
• Adafruit Ultimate GPS ($40)
• 1 PPS output for time sync
Radio Interface CAT control to radio • USB-to-serial adapter
• CI-V interface (Icom)
• Hamlib compatible
Display (Optional) Show current status • 16x2 LCD ($5)
• OLED display ($10)
• Touch screen ($30)
Rotator Interface (Optional) Control antenna pointing • Relay board for rotator control
• RS-232 interface (Yaesu)
• USB adapter for SPID

Software Architecture:

  1. TLE Management:
    • Download and parse TLEs automatically
    • Update daily from Celestrak
    • Store locally with timestamp
  2. Real-Time Propagation:
    • Use GPS time for accurate calculations
    • Implement SGP4 propagator
    • Calculate satellite position every 1-2 seconds
    • Convert to topocentric coordinates (Az/El)
  3. Doppler Calculation:
    • Compute radial velocity from position/velocity vectors
    • Calculate frequency shift: Δf = (v/c) × f₀
    • Apply to both uplink and downlink frequencies
    • Account for frequency inversion on transponders
  4. Radio Control:
    • Connect via CAT interface (serial or USB)
    • Send frequency commands in radio-specific format
    • Update VFO-A (RX) and VFO-B (TX) independently
    • Adjust every 1-2 seconds for smooth tracking
    • Handle radio errors gracefully
  5. Rotator Control (Optional):
    • Calculate azimuth and elevation commands
    • Send to rotator controller
    • Update every 5-10 seconds (slower than frequency)
    • Implement soft stops to prevent mechanical damage
  6. User Interface:
    • Display current satellite position and signal path
    • Show active frequency corrections
    • Countdown to AOS/TCA/LOS
    • Manual override capability
    • Pass logging and statistics

Implementation Example (Python + Hamlib):

import Hamlib # Radio control library import ephem import time import serial # For GPS communication class DopplerController: def __init__(self, radio_model, radio_port, gps_port): # Initialize radio control Hamlib.rig_set_debug(Hamlib.RIG_DEBUG_NONE) self.rig = Hamlib.Rig(radio_model) self.rig.state.rigport.pathname = radio_port self.rig.open() # Initialize GPS self.gps = serial.Serial(gps_port, 9600, timeout=1) # Observer location (will be updated from GPS) self.observer = ephem.Observer() self.observer.lat = '5.603' # Default: Accra self.observer.lon = '-0.187' self.observer.elevation = 61 def calculate_doppler(self, satellite, freq_mhz): """Calculate Doppler-shifted frequency""" # Update satellite position self.observer.date = ephem.now() satellite.compute(self.observer) # Get satellite velocity (range rate in m/s) range_rate = satellite.range_velocity # meters/second # Calculate Doppler shift c = 299792458 # speed of light (m/s) doppler_shift_mhz = (range_rate / c) * freq_mhz # Corrected frequency corrected_freq = freq_mhz + doppler_shift_mhz return corrected_freq, doppler_shift_mhz def set_frequency(self, freq_hz, vfo=Hamlib.RIG_VFO_A): """Set radio frequency via CAT""" try: self.rig.set_freq(vfo, int(freq_hz)) return True except Exception as e: print(f"Error setting frequency: {e}") return False def track_satellite(self, satellite, downlink_mhz, uplink_mhz): """Track satellite and correct Doppler continuously""" print(f"Starting Doppler tracking for {satellite.name}") while True: # Calculate Doppler-corrected frequencies rx_freq, rx_doppler = self.calculate_doppler(satellite, downlink_mhz) tx_freq, tx_doppler = self.calculate_doppler(satellite, uplink_mhz) # Uplink Doppler is inverted (pre-compensate) tx_freq = uplink_mhz - tx_doppler # Update radio frequencies self.set_frequency(rx_freq * 1e6, Hamlib.RIG_VFO_A) # RX self.set_frequency(tx_freq * 1e6, Hamlib.RIG_VFO_B) # TX # Display current status print(f"RX: {rx_freq:.6f} MHz (Doppler: {rx_doppler*1000:+.2f} kHz)") print(f"TX: {tx_freq:.6f} MHz (Doppler: {-tx_doppler*1000:+.2f} kHz)") print(f"Elevation: {float(satellite.alt)*180/ephem.pi:.1f}°\n") # Update every 2 seconds time.sleep(2) # Check if satellite has set if float(satellite.alt) < 0: print("Satellite has set. Ending tracking.") break def close(self): """Clean up resources""" self.rig.close() self.gps.close() # Usage example controller = DopplerController( radio_model=Hamlib.RIG_MODEL_IC9700, # Icom IC-9700 radio_port='/dev/ttyUSB0', gps_port='/dev/ttyUSB1' ) # Load satellite TLE so124 = ephem.readtle( "SO-124", "1 62690U 25009CK 25345.78916667 .00001234 00000-0 54321-4 0 9998", "2 62690 97.5123 265.4321 0009234 62.1234 298.5678 14.92345678901234" ) # Track satellite with Doppler correction controller.track_satellite( satellite=so124, downlink_mhz=436.885, # SO-124 downlink uplink_mhz=145.925 # SO-124 uplink ) controller.close()

Testing & Calibration:

  • Test with known satellite (SO-124 or ISS)
  • Compare manual tuning vs. automated
  • Verify frequency accuracy (within ±500 Hz)
  • Check update rate (should be smooth, not jumpy)
  • Test error handling (what if radio disconnects?)
  • Validate GPS time synchronization

Project 3: Satellite Signal Strength Data Logger

Objective:

Record received signal strength (RSSI) throughout satellite passes to create signal profiles, analyze propagation effects, and validate theoretical models.

Data Collection Requirements:

Parameter Source Sampling Rate
Timestamp GPS receiver (PPS sync) Every record
Satellite Position SGP4 calculation from TLE 1 Hz
Azimuth & Elevation Observer-relative coordinates 1 Hz
Range (Distance) Calculated from position vectors 1 Hz
Receive Frequency Radio CAT interface 1 Hz
Signal Strength (RSSI) Radio S-meter or SDR measurement 2-10 Hz
Weather Data Temperature, humidity, pressure sensors 0.1 Hz (every 10 sec)
Antenna Polarization Manual entry or rotator feedback On change

Hardware Setup:

  • SDR Receiver: RTL-SDR, Airspy, or HackRF
    • Advantage: Direct RSSI measurement in software
    • Can log raw IQ samples for offline analysis
    • FFT provides spectrum data
  • Alternative: Radio with CAT S-meter reading
    • Query S-meter value via CAT commands
    • Convert S-units to dBm (approx: S9 = -73 dBm, +6dB per S-unit above S9)
  • Weather Station: BME280 sensor or similar
    • Temperature: ±1°C accuracy
    • Humidity: ±3% RH
    • Barometric pressure: ±1 hPa
    • Connect via I2C to microcontroller

Analysis Tasks:

  1. Signal Strength vs. Elevation Profile:
    • Plot RSSI vs. elevation angle
    • Expected: Signal strongest at TCA (highest elevation)
    • Observe: How much does signal vary with elevation?
    • Compare multiple passes for consistency
  2. Free-Space Path Loss Validation:
    • Calculate theoretical FSPL: FSPL(dB) = 32.45 + 20log(f_MHz) + 20log(d_km)
    • Compare measured signal strength to prediction
    • Account for antenna gains and cable losses
    • Identify discrepancies (atmospheric effects, multipath, etc.)
  3. Faraday Rotation Effects:
    • If using linear polarization, observe signal fading
    • Fading occurs when satellite polarization rotates relative to ground antenna
    • Can cause 20-30 dB signal loss
    • Circular polarization eliminates this issue (verify with data)
  4. Ionospheric Scintillation:
    • Look for rapid signal fluctuations (scintillation)
    • More common at low elevations, near horizon
    • More pronounced at higher solar activity
    • Correlate with ionospheric conditions (spaceweather.gov)
  5. Weather Correlation:
    • Does signal strength vary with atmospheric conditions?
    • Compare clear sky vs. overcast vs. rain
    • VHF/UHF mostly unaffected by weather (unlike Ku-band satellite TV)
    • Heavy rain may cause slight attenuation (<1 dB typically)
  6. Satellite Comparison:
    • Compare different satellites at same elevation
    • SO-124 vs. SO-50: Which has stronger signal?
    • Differences due to transmit power, antenna pattern, age/degradation

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt import pandas as pd import numpy as np # Load logged data data = pd.read_csv('so124_pass_20251215.csv') # Create figure with subplots fig, axes = plt.subplots(2, 2, figsize=(12, 10)) # Plot 1: Signal Strength vs. Elevation axes[0, 0].plot(data['elevation'], data['rssi_dbm'], 'b-') axes[0, 0].set_xlabel('Elevation (degrees)') axes[0, 0].set_ylabel('Signal Strength (dBm)') axes[0, 0].set_title('Signal Strength vs. Elevation') # Plot 2: Free-Space Path Loss Comparison axes[0, 1].plot(data['range_km'], data['rssi_dbm'], 'ro', label='Measured') # Calculate theoretical FSPL freq_mhz = 436.885 fspl = 32.45 + 20*np.log10(freq_mhz) + 20*np.log10(data['range_km']) # Convert to relative signal strength (simplified) theoretical_rssi = -fspl + 100 # Arbitrary offset for comparison axes[0, 1].plot(data['range_km'], theoretical_rssi, 'b-', label='Theoretical FSPL') axes[0, 1].set_xlabel('Range (km)') axes[0, 1].set_ylabel('Signal Strength (dBm)') axes[0, 1].set_title('FSPL Validation') axes[0, 1].legend() # Plot 3: Doppler Shift Profile axes[1, 0].plot(data['time_min'], data['doppler_shift_hz']/1000, 'g-') axes[1, 0].set_xlabel('Time from AOS (minutes)') axes[1, 0].set_ylabel('Doppler Shift (kHz)') axes[1, 0].set_title('Doppler Shift Profile') axes[1, 0].axhline(y=0, color='k', linestyle='--', alpha=0.3) # Plot 4: Signal Strength vs. Time axes[1, 1].plot(data['time_min'], data['rssi_dbm'], 'm-') axes[1, 1].set_xlabel('Time from AOS (minutes)') axes[1, 1].set_ylabel('Signal Strength (dBm)') axes[1, 1].set_title('Signal Strength Timeline') # Mark TCA tca_index = data['elevation'].idxmax() axes[1, 1].axvline(x=data.loc[tca_index, 'time_min'], color='r', linestyle='--', alpha=0.5, label='TCA') axes[1, 1].legend() plt.tight_layout() plt.show()
